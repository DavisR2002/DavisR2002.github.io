1.2: Accountability, Authenication and Authorization


Beyond the CIA Triad are some other concepts that are essential for any aspiring digital security specialist to be familiar with. These are the concepts of Accountability, Authenication and Authorization. Accountability refers to the concept of knowing the responsibilities of a person or process. It is essential to understand where responsibilities lie when designing a security system, as each element of it will (ideally) be made to prevent, detect or limit threats.

Authenication is a word used to describe the process of determining who or what something is. In a security setting, this can be achieved in multiple ways. Examples include biometric scanning, employee keycards or staff login details. These are all measures put in place to ensure that people who want to access important things are who they say they are.

Authorization describes the process of determining whether someone is allowed to gain access to something. This is just as important as authenticating somebody, if not more important, as to authorize a person means to allow them access to whatever it is they are looking for.

The three A's (as a way to group these terms) can be seen in action throughout the digital world. Email verification is a ubiquitous example of both authentication and authorization - the user enters their email to authenticate themselves as a real person, and is then authorized to access the service they wish to use. In this situation, it is the verification service's responsibility to ensure that the authentication process is correct.
